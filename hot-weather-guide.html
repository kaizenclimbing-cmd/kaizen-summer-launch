Climbing in the Heat

Hot temps can make you feel slow and sluggish. Sometimes it's a good chance to take it easy — but if you want to push on, here's what I've learnt from years of climbing and training in South East Asia.

First, drop the ego

Accept that your performance is going to be down. As long as things feel hard and you're training at the appropriate level, you're all good.

It's the stimulus that counts, not the numbers.

The tricks
01
Time it right

Train early in the day or later in the evening — and avoid climbing in the sun at all costs. Even in the dead of winter this makes things harder.

02
Hydrate properly

Stay hydrated, and maybe even add some salts/electrolytes. You're losing more than water when you're sweating through a session.

03
Shorten your warm-up

Your body is already warm, so you don't need as much as you would on a cold winter's day. Save the energy for the actual climbing.

04
Use fans

Point them at yourself, at the wall, or on your hands. Fans on your skin and the holds/fingerboard keep skin dry and temps down.

05
Cool your skin between goes

Hold a cold rock, waft your hands in the air, and rest between climbs. Some even like to soak their hands in cold water.

06
Climb on wood

MoonBoard, woody spray walls or hangboards — not comp-style plastic — when it's hot. Wood handles sweaty skin far better.

07
Keep it simple

Short boulders > long endurance when feet, skin and brain are cooked.

08
Sort your chalk

Liquid chalk can be a saviour — and some even keep their chalk bag in a cooler.

09
Size your shoes up

Go a touch bigger/softer — your feet will swell and hurt in the heat.

10
Have a plan B

If it's all still too much on the brain and skin: fingerboard + lifting weights shouldn't be impacted much by the heat, and will let you work hard when your fingers and brain feel like mush.

Bonus: commercial gyms can also have aircon.
