The 5 Habits of Elite Climbers

I'm Buster — climbing coach, and the guy from the Hannah Morris video. I work with climbers at every level, from people a few months in through to Olympic athletes and 9B sport climbers.

This short course covers the five habits I see in almost every elite climber I've worked with. None of them are secrets — but most climbers never apply them consistently. Each lesson takes about two minutes to read, and each one is something you can use in your very next session.

Lesson 1 of 5

Fingerboarding

In climbing, your fingers are your connection to the rock — so it's no surprise that finger strength is the one attribute the strongest climbers share.

The question is: how do you build it efficiently?

Climbing on hard holds helps, but it only goes so far. The real key is adding structure. That's where fingerboarding comes in. It's targeted, measurable, and intense — which is exactly why you'll see elite climbers doing it at the start of every session, almost without exception.

The protocol

  • Warm up gradually, starting with easier holds and building up
  • Find a level where a 10-second half-crimp hang feels genuinely hard — but you could hold on for a couple more seconds if you had to
  • Do 3 sets with 2–3 minutes rest between (proper rest — don't rush it)
  • Adjust the difficulty by changing holds, adding weight, or reducing hand size

That's it. Short, focused, and done before you get distracted. It'll warm your fingers up properly, lower your injury risk, and give you a clear baseline to build from.

Give it a go next session. Next up: why the pros are intentional about every session, and how you can be too.

Lesson 2 of 5

Intention

The difference between climbers who improve and climbers who plateau comes down to one thing: intention.

Not talent. Not how often they climb. Intention.

Training = a goal, a plan, and a purpose for each session.
Exercise = showing up and doing whatever feels good on the day.

Both are fine — but only one will reliably move you forward.

Three levels of intention

  • Long-term intention — committing to a process, not just chasing the next grade
  • Session intention — giving each session a specific focus or theme
  • Move intention — paying attention to how and why you're moving the way you are

The pros are good at all three. But you can start small. Before your next session, just pick one thing:

"Today I'm going to work on my footwork."
"This session I'll focus on reading the moves before I get on the wall."
"I'm going to try to keep my hips closer to the rock."

One small intention, applied consistently, compounds fast. Footwork is a great place to start — I work on it at the beginning of most of my own sessions.
Lesson 3 of 5

Limit bouldering

The single thing I prioritise most in my own training, and that I give to almost every coaching client: limit bouldering.

And no — I don't mean six-hour sessions where you thrash yourself on every hard climb in the gym and end up wrecked. That's not training, that's just suffering.

I mean intentional limit bouldering. High-quality, focused attempts on problems at or just above your limit. Problems that will take some working out. Problems you might not even complete — and that's fine.

How to do it

  • Pick 3 problems at or just above your max grade
  • Spend 20–30 minutes working each one
  • Rest properly between attempts — take your shoes off, brush the holds, use a timer if it helps
  • Break the problem into sections and work individual moves, not just full-length attempts
  • Film yourself. Watch it back. Look at your feet, your hips, your timing.

The key is quality over quantity. You want to be trying at your actual maximum — which means lots of rest between goes, and finishing while you still feel strong.

It might not feel like a hard session. There's a lot of sitting around. That's normal. Over time the gains in power, efficiency, and movement reading will be significant.

The goal isn't to top the boulder. The goal is to train. High-quality efforts are the point.
Lesson 4 of 5

Climbing with others

You probably already know that climbing with others is more enjoyable than climbing solo. But it's also one of the most underrated training tools there is.

Watch any elite climbing team — the Japanese in particular — and you'll notice they always train together. In a group. Watching each other, sharing beta, trying things they'd never have attempted on their own. That's not just a cultural thing. It's smart.

When you climb with other people, you get an objective view of your own climbing that's almost impossible to get alone. Different body types, strengths, and movement styles will show you sequences you'd never have found. Someone will try a boulder in a way that makes you think: I wouldn't have done it like that — and sometimes that's exactly the insight you were missing.

It works both ways. Climbing with someone stronger pulls you up. Climbing with someone newer forces you to articulate what you know.

Make the most of it

  • Try problems you'd normally walk past because a friend has a go at them
  • Film each other and watch it back together
  • Ask: "How would you do this differently?"
  • If someone climbs a problem in a style that doesn't suit you — try copying it anyway

Next time you're at the wall, treat the people around you as a resource. You'll learn a surprising amount just by paying attention.

Lesson 5 of 5

Strength training

Most climbers are put off by strength training off the wall. I get it. It doesn't feel specific to climbing. It doesn't feel like practice. And when you want to improve, the obvious answer seems like: just climb more.

But here's why I recommend it to almost every client I work with.

It makes you stronger in ways climbing alone can't. Climbing loads your body in very specific patterns. Weights load it in controlled, progressive ways that complement those patterns — building a stronger, more resilient climber overall.

It significantly reduces injury risk. When your muscles are conditioned to handle loads that exceed what you'd encounter on the wall, climbing becomes relatively easy on the body. You recover faster between sessions and the risk of overuse injuries goes down.

It gives you more climbing time. Counter-intuitive, but true. A stronger, better-conditioned body handles the demands of climbing more easily — which means you can climb more, for longer, and recover better. More quality practice time. Faster progress.

You don't need to become a weightlifter

  • Pull-ups and weighted pull-ups
  • Single-arm rows
  • Dead hangs with added weight
  • Core work (hollow body holds, L-sits)

Two or three short sessions a week is enough. It'll pay off on the wall.

Course complete

The five habits — and what comes next

That's the framework. The five habits I see in almost every elite climber I've worked with or watched closely:

  1. Fingerboarding — short, targeted, done consistently
  2. Session intention — every session has a focus
  3. Limit bouldering — quality efforts, proper rest
  4. Climbing with others — learning from the people around you
  5. Strength training — building a body that supports your climbing

Knowing this stuff is a solid start — most climbers don't even have this framework. But the gap between knowing and actually doing it consistently is where progress stalls. Life gets busy. Sessions drift. The plan fades out.
